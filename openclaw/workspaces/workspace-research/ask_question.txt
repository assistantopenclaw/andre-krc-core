CAPTION: Most men have never been taught to ask this question.


You have a thought. It's dark, it's heavy, it tells you something is wrong with you — that you're a fraud, that she'll leave, that you'll fail, that you don't deserve what you're trying to build. And you assume it's you. Your brain. Your damage.


What if it's not?


There's a simple test. One question that separates what's coming from God from what's coming from somewhere else:


Is this thought pulling me closer to God, or separating me from Him?


That's it. That's the whole test.


Thoughts from God — even the hard ones, even the convicting ones — produce movement. They make you want to repent, or grow, or show up differently. They increase your trust in God even when they're uncomfortable.


Thoughts from the enemy do one thing: they separate. They tell you God is far. They tell you that you've gone too far. They make you feel like you're uniquely broken — unlike anyone else — and that no one, including God, is available for what you're dealing with.


You've been listening to that voice and thinking it was your own conscience. You've built a whole self-image around what a demon kept telling you about yourself.


It wasn't your thought. It was a conversation you didn't know you were in.


Run the test. Ask the question. If the thought separates you from God and kills your trust, it didn't come from the one who made you.


Reject it on sight.

POST_ID: POST_RUN_20260227_MeEeshZH_003 OVERLAY_HOOK: God isn't silent. Your question is just too vague for a specific answer. OVERLAY_CTA: read caption


CAPTION: Most people pray in headlines.


"God, give me clarity." "God, show me the path." "God, help me with this relationship." And then they wait. And nothing comes. Or at least — nothing they can act on. And they conclude that either God isn't listening, or His answer is "not yet," or they're just not spiritual enough to hear.


The problem usually isn't any of those things.


The problem is that you asked a vague question. And vague questions get vague answers.


Think about it this way: if you ask an expert consultant "what should I do with my life," they can't give you a blueprint. But if you ask "what should I do when she shuts down and goes cold — specifically, in those next fifteen minutes?" — now there's something to work with. Now an answer can actually land.


God is not a vending machine and prayer is not a slot pull. But He is also not a fortune teller dropping riddles into your life and hoping you piece them together. He is a counselor. And every good counselor will tell you: the quality of the answer depends entirely on the quality of the question.


The clearer the ask, the clearer the answer. The more defined the problem, the more actionable the guidance.


This week, try it. Don't pray for "peace." Pray about the specific moment that stole your peace. Don't ask for "wisdom." Ask about the specific decision sitting in front of you right now.


Watch what happens when you stop praying in headlines and start praying like you're in a conversation.


He was always listening. You just weren't asking precisely enough for the answer to land.
